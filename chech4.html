Ch4-Structure Of The Atom

Introduction

One of the first indications that atoms were not indivisible comes from the studying of static electricity and the conditions under which electricity is conducted by different substances.

Charged particles in matter

It was known by 1900 that atoms contained atleast one sub-atomic particle- The electron identified by J.J. Thomson. Even before that E. Goldetein in 1886 dicovered the precence of new radiations in a gas discharge and called them canal rays. These rays were positively charged and were later identified as protons. This sub-atomic particle had a charge equal in magnitude but opposite in sign to that of an electron. Its mass was approximately 2000 times that of an electron.
An electron is represented as e⁻ and a proton is represented as p⁺. The mass of an proton is taken as one unit and its charge as plus one. The mass of an electron is considered to be negligible and its charge is minus one.

Drawback of Dalton's Atomic Theory

Dalton's atomic theory was based on the idea that atoms were indivisible. The discovery of sub-atomic particles proved that atoms were divisible and led to the failure of that idea of Dalton's atomic theory.

Thomson's Model of an Atom

Thomson proposed a model of an atom in which electrons were embedded in a sphere of positive charge, like a "christmas pudding" or "seeds in a watermelon" model. Thomson proposed that:
  • An atom consists of a positively charged sphere and the electrons are embedded in it.
  • The negative and positive charges are equal in magnitude. So, the atom as a whole is electrically neutral.

Rutherford's Model of an Atom

Earnest Rutherford designed an experiment for this, in that expriment fast moving alpha(α)-particles were made to fall on a thin gold foil.
  • He selected a gold foil because he wanted as thin a layer as possible. This gold foil was about 1000 atoms thick.
  • α-particles are doubly-charged helium ions. Since they have a mass of 4u, the fast-moving α-particles have a considerable amount of energy.
  • It was expected that α-particles would be deflected by the sub-atomic particles in the gold atoms. Since the α-particles were much heavier than the protons he didn't expect to see large deflections.
But the α-particle scattering experiment gave some unexpected results:
  • Most of the fast moving α-particles passed straight through the gold foil.
  • A few α-particles were deflected by small angles.
  • Suprisingly one out of every 12000 particles appeared to rebound.
following a similar reasoning, Rutherford concluded that:
  • Most of the space inside the atom is empty, since most of the α-particles passed straight through the gold foil.
  • Very few particles were deflected from thir path, indicating that the positive charge of an atom occupies very little space.
  • A very small fraction of α-particles were deflected by 180°, indicating that all the positive charge and the mass of the gold atom were Concentrated in a very small volume within the atom.
The radius of the nucleus is about 10⁵ less than the radius of the atom. On the basis of these observations, Rutherford proposed a new model of an atom in which:
  • There is a positively charged centre in an atom called the nucleus. Nearly all the mass an atom resides in the nucleus.
  • The electrons revolve around the nucleus in circular paths.
  • The size of the nucleus is very small compared to the size of the atom.

Drawback of Rutherford's Model of an Atom

The revolution of the electron in a circular orbit is not expected to be stable, as a charged electron moving in a circular orbit would undergo acceleration and would constantly lose energy by emitting radiation and finally fall into the nucleus. if this were so, the atom should be unstable.

Bohr's Model of an Atom

In order to overcome the drawback of Rutherford's model, Niels Bohr put forward the following postulates:
  • Only certain special orbits known as discrete orbits of electrons are allowed inside the atom.
  • While revolving in discrete orbits the electrons don't radiate energy.
The orbits or shells are called energy levels. These orbits or shells are represented by the letters K, L, M, N... or the numbers 1, 2, 3, 4...

Neutrons

  • In 1932, James Chadwick discovered a new sub-atomic particle called the neutron.
  • A neutron is an uncharged particle having a mass nearly equal to that of a proton.
  • Neutrons are present in the nucleus of all atoms, except hydrogen in general.
  • Neutrons are represented as n.
  • The mass of an atom is given by the sum of the masses of protons and neutrons in the nucleus.

How are electrons distributed?

The distribution of electrons into different orbits of an atom was suggested by Bohr and Bury.
The following rules are used to determine the distribution of electrons in different orbits:
  • The maximum number of electrons in any orbit is given by the formula 2n², where n is the orbit number. Hence the maximum number of electrons in different orbits are as follows:
    First orbit (K) = 2(1)² = 2
    Second orbit (L) = 2(2)² = 8
    Third orbit (M) = 2(3)² = 18
    Fourth orbit (N) = 2(4)² = 32 and so on.
  • The maximum number of electrons that can be accommodated in the outermost orbit is 8.
  • Electrons are not accommodated in an orbit unless the inner orbits are filled. That is, the orbits are filled in a stepwise manner.

Valency

The valency of an element is the number of electrons that it can lose, gain or share to complete its outermost shell.

Atomic Number and Mass Number

Atomic Number

The atomic number of an element is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom of that element.
  • It is the number of protons of an, which determines its atomic number.
  • It is denoted by 'Z'.
  • The atomic number is the total number of protons present in the nucleus of an atom.

Mass Number

The mass number of an element is the sum of the number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom of that element.
  • It is denoted by 'A'.

Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same atomic number but different mass numbers. Example: hydrogen has three isotopes: protium(¹H), deuterium(²H or D), and tritium(³H or T). The atomic number of each one is 1.
Other such examples include carbon-12(¹²C) and carbon-14(¹⁴C) both of them have the same atomic number 6. Chlorine occurs in nature in two isotopes: chlorine-35(³⁵Cl) and chlorine-37(³⁷Cl). obviously the question arises: What should we take as the mass of Chlorine atom? The average atomic mass of chlorine is calculated as follows:
  • mass₁ X abundance percentage₁ + mass₂ X abundance percentage₂
  • 35 X ( 75/100 ) + 37 X ( 25/100 ) = 35.5
Applications of Isotopes
  • An isotope of uranium is used as a fuel in nuclear reactors.
  • An isotope of cobalt is used in the treatment of cancer.
  • An isotope of iodine is used in the treatment of goitre.

Isobars

Isobars are atoms of different elements that have the same mass number but different atomic numbers.
